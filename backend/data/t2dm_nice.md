First-line pharmacological treatment for adults with type 2 diabetes is standard-release metformin. Metformin should be offered to adults with type 2 diabetes if lifestyle measures alone do not keep HbA1c below the agreed threshold. Start at a low dose and titrate gradually to reduce the risk of gastrointestinal side effects.

In adults with type 2 diabetes and chronic kidney disease, review the dose of metformin if the estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) is below 45 mL/min/1.73m^2, and stop metformin if the eGFR is below 30 mL/min/1.73m^2. A modified-release preparation may be tried if gastrointestinal tolerability prevents continued use of standard-release metformin.

For adults with type 2 diabetes who have chronic heart failure or established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, consider adding a sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 (SGLT2) inhibitor with proven cardiovascular benefit to metformin. For those at high risk of cardiovascular disease, an SGLT2 inhibitor should also be considered. SGLT2 inhibitors reduce the risk of hospitalisation for heart failure and slow progression of diabetic kidney disease.

If metformin is contraindicated or not tolerated, consider initial drug treatment with a dipeptidyl peptidase-4 (DPP-4) inhibitor, pioglitazone, a sulfonylurea, or an SGLT2 inhibitor, taking into account cardiovascular and renal status. Rescue therapy with insulin should be considered at any time if a person is symptomatically hyperglycaemic.
