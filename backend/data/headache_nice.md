A headache is pain felt anywhere in the head or upper neck. Most headaches are primary headaches, meaning they are not caused by an underlying disease. The common primary headaches are tension-type headache, migraine, and cluster headache. Secondary headaches are caused by another condition such as infection, medication overuse, raised blood pressure, or, rarely, serious causes such as bleeding in the brain.

Tension-type headache is the most common type. It usually causes a mild to moderate, bilateral, pressing or tightening pain (like a band around the head) that is not made worse by routine physical activity and is not associated with nausea. First-line management for an acute tension-type headache is a simple analgesic such as paracetamol or a non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) like ibuprofen or aspirin. Advise people to use acute painkillers on no more than 2 days per week to avoid medication-overuse headache. Non-drug measures include adequate hydration, regular meals and sleep, reducing stress, limiting screen time, and managing caffeine intake.

Migraine typically causes a moderate to severe, often one-sided, throbbing headache that is worsened by activity and is commonly associated with nausea, vomiting, or sensitivity to light and sound. Acute treatment options include an NSAID or aspirin, and a triptan such as sumatriptan for moderate to severe attacks. Anti-sickness medicines can be added. People with frequent migraines may be offered preventive treatment such as propranolol or topiramate.

Seek urgent medical attention for any headache that is sudden and severe ("thunderclap"), or is accompanied by fever and a stiff neck, a rash, confusion, weakness, visual loss, a head injury, or that progressively worsens over days. These are red-flag features that may indicate a serious secondary cause and require same-day assessment. Routine simple headaches that respond to rest and over-the-counter painkillers do not usually need investigation.
