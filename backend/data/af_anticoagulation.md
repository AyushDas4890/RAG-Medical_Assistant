title: Atrial fibrillation — diagnosis and management

Assess stroke risk in people with atrial fibrillation using the CHA2DS2-VASc score, and assess bleeding risk using the ORBIT bleeding score. Offer anticoagulation to people with a CHA2DS2-VASc score of 2 or above, taking bleeding risk into account. Consider anticoagulation for men with a CHA2DS2-VASc score of 1. Do not offer anticoagulation solely on the basis of age.

Offer a direct-acting oral anticoagulant (DOAC) — apixaban, dabigatran, edoxaban, or rivaroxaban — as first-line anticoagulation for people with non-valvular atrial fibrillation. If a DOAC is contraindicated, not tolerated, or unsuitable, offer a vitamin K antagonist such as warfarin. Do not offer aspirin monotherapy solely for stroke prevention in atrial fibrillation.

Doses of DOACs must be adjusted for renal function, age, and body weight according to the product characteristics; for example, apixaban dose reduction is considered when at least two of the following are present: age 80 years or older, body weight 60 kg or less, or serum creatinine 133 micromol/L or more. Review the need for and quality of anticoagulation at least annually, including renal function for people taking a DOAC.

Do not withhold anticoagulation solely because the person is at risk of falls. Before starting, discuss the benefits of stroke prevention against the risks of bleeding, and support the person to make an informed decision.
